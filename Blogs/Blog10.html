For me to actually begin analysing required extensive amounts of reading of the actually text but still nothing.With all hope lost a found a , Following a definition by BCC, Close reading is a way of analyzing literature that pays close attention to the details within a passage or text. By examining these elements carefully, the reader uncovers deeper meanings. The insights gained from this process reflect the reader’s own understanding or interpretation of the text. Kasapis - 2020 - Internet Access is a Fundamental Right

"happiness is a human right. Lack of food, education, health care and fair working conditions are obstacles to achieving happiness. Therefore, all of the above are - like happiness human rights." [1]
"free internet access should be indispensable to all members of an organized society." [1]

In this discussion , it begins with Rezis Debre, Chilean president Salvador Allende cited for his belief that "happiness is a human right" in the article's first paragraph of text.He opens by defining what a right is and what a right entails preparing a trailway to add his own opinion and requirement of what a right should be.He argues that essential needs such as food, education, healthcare, and fair working conditions are requirements for achieving happiness and any type of fulfillment . Therefore he argues these basic needs could be considered as human rights as well constituting it from the fact that happiness is a right. Kasapis attempts to create a comparative analysis between basic necessities and access to the internet, suggesting that basic essentials are required for a dignified and fulfilled life, so is access to the internet in the digital world. Hence reinforcing his point that free internet should be available everywhere and to everyone. Which then raises the question, why is not taken seriously as it is, why is not considered a right officially, what are the constraints stopping this ideal from advancing.

“So those who do not have access to the internet cannot participate in this new kind of digital economy that is flourishing. This means that not only can they not buy or sell goods, but that they cannot "sell" their work;”[1]

This discussion continues further in which the debate about treating internet access as a human right brings about explanations and reasons to treat it as such.It is argued that internet access plays a role in enabling individuals to exercise the basic fundamental rights, as it serves as a gateway to essential services and economic opportunities.The author elaborates and expresses a rise in digital inequality which inversely cause an increase in economic inequality. He highlights a comparative analysis between the immense online wealth amassed by certain individuals and alludes to the fact that most of the global population lacks internet access hence are unable to participate in possibly acquiring mass wealth. This is referred to as digital exclusion which prevents millions from participating in a new modern society, restricting them from services and opportunities in education, healthcare, and employment. Raising the question, how do we come up with solutions to this type of dilemma.The need of internet access becomes more and more potent.

“Should all citizens have access to the internet - regardless of income - and if so, are governments supposed to take care of it?” [1]
“What should be the rules governing the internet and who will set them?” [1]

He ends his argument with questions that help us reflect on deeper concerns about equity, participation, and governance in the digital era. The author elaborates that the mission of the institute is to help answer these questions by promoting a fair and democratic digital society, one that is built on principles of a new defined human rights system. Taking internet access as any social or political benefit. It should be something everyone serves as a contributor but also listener and engager instead of solely relying on governance as its control factor, hopefully creating a future far from disparity.
