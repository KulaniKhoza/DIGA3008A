Blog 9: Evaluation of Kasapis and Tataki & Glynos

21 April 2025, 23:00

Kasapis Evaluation

Kasapis’s article is a call to action for governments, institutions, and people to rethink how we view internet access. He says it should be seen as a basic human right, not just something extra or a luxury. Access to the internet is important because it affects people’s dignity and their ability to participate in many parts of modern life.

During the Covid-19 pandemic, many schools and workplaces closed to prevent the virus from spreading. People had to work, learn, and socialize from home using the internet. This made it clear that those without internet access faced serious problems. They were left out of important activities, which made their lives harder.

Kasapis points out that not having internet access puts people at a big disadvantage. This is not just a problem for individuals, but for society as a whole. When many people cannot connect online, the gap between rich and poor grows larger. This digital divide means some people cannot take part in education, work, healthcare, and other essential services.

Kasapis also explains that as society becomes more digital, it is very important to make sure everyone has fair access to the internet. This helps promote social justice, allows people to join the economy, and supports personal and community development.

Finally, Kasapis argues that internet access should be treated like other basic human rights because it helps improve society overall. If we ignore this right, more people will be excluded and society will suffer. But if we ensure fair and free internet access for all, we can help create a more equal and connected world.

Tataki and Glynos(2020) - Digital Divide Widens

“Digital Divide Widens” by Tataki and Glynos brought to light the profound and persistent inequalities embedded in our digital world,inequalities that the COVID-19 pandemic brought to our reality. The reality of covid brought extensified the fact that not everyone has access to interact with digital world, while many of us had the privilege of entering the schooling and working sector through zoom meetings and so on,A large reflection of people and students did not have access to any type of online which lingers a sense unfairness and disparity for the future of education and and the future of the youth as a whole.

The article made me reflect on the privileges often taken for granted such as having a computer, stable Internet, and a quiet space to work or study. It also highlighted how digital access is not a luxury but a fundamental need similar to Kasapis viewings , especially in times of crisis. The authors’ point that digital infrastructure must be treated as essential public infrastructure, like electricity or water, resonated deeply with me. It’s not just about helping people catch up during a pandemic; it’s about ensuring everyone can participate in a rapidly digitizing world.

I particularly found sections discussing how the digital divide disproportionately affects girls, low-income families, and those in the Global South interesting and disheartening. These insights emphasized how digital exclusion intersects with broader social injustices, reinforcing patterns of inequality. The temporary measures some governments implemented stemming from handing out laptops or free data felt like ploys to show people that “doing” something.They display lack of responsibility to acknowledge the depth of the situation as well as its severity.

Ultimately, this article made me think critically about the role of policy, technology, and collective responsibility in shaping a fairer digital future. If we fail to act now, we risk creating a world where access to opportunity is determined not just by socioeconomic background, but by digital connectivity. The authors’ call to treat digital inclusion as a long-term priority, beyond the pandemic,is an urgent call to justice. This has made me aware of the situation arising in our global economy.

References:
