Blog 13: An Ethical Internet

19 May 2025, 17:00

The Internet as a moral space

When I consider what an ethical internet looks like to me, I compare it to my previous view on ethics in general, alluding as a matter of simply enforcing rules,restricting harmful content(protecting users) and adjusting technical/cookie settings. Rather, I have come to understand the internet as a moral environment,a space that carries with it the weight of ethical responsibility. Inspired by the work of media theorist Roger Silverstone and the reflections of Shani Orgad in “The Internet as a Moral Space”, I now view the internet not just as a tool for interaction, but as a place where decisions about what people can see, who can use a site, and how they can interact with it affect the lives of others. We as people never know the reach we have on various audiences hence a need of caution is required.

My own development as a Web developer has derived a familiar path. I began by focusing on structure, user experience, and functionality. I wanted my website to run efficiently, look clean, and be easy to navigate. Initially, it was built as a series of HTML pages, but as I introduced CSS and JavaScript to manage dynamic content such as an automatic navigation bar and interactive blog listings,I realized that my design decisions were also social and ethical ones. Each time I made the interface more responsive or the navigation more intuitive, I was doing more than improving usability. I was shaping how others experienced the space/my website, and by extension, how they felt seen by my design, how they felt included and or excluded.Essentially i was navigating their interactivity and user experience to what i would derive as a “ethical internet” Shaping their emotions in either a positive or negative way.

Reading Silverstone’s work added a deeper layer to this my thinking and definition to an ethical internet. He argued that the media of which the internet is now an inseparable part constitute a moral space, one where the quality of our relationships with others, particularly distant relations(people we relatively don't know), is continuously negotiated. One of his most interesting contributions is the concept of “proper distance,” the idea that ethical relationships require a balance in a sense that it is close enough to acknowledge and care for the other, yet distant enough to respect their difference. In digital spaces, where proximity is easily faked and connection can be superficial and unreal, this idea challenges the dominant view that more interaction automatically means deeper engagement making us question it ethically as a whole.

Silverstone criticises the kind of connectivity and concept of relationship the internet often promotes,he emphasises on the manner social networks and algorithms bring people closer and make differences less visible, but this doesn't always encourage people to act responsibly. As Shani Orgad explains in her interpretation of his work,explaining that the problem isn’t how far apart people are online, but that we often don’t feel responsible for others we interact with from a distance. This feels especially true today, where being “connected” doesn’t always mean we understand, care about, or respect each other hence bringing back the fact that interactivity feels superficial and fake in relation to the internet. In fact, the architecture of many online platforms seems to encourage superficial engagement for example: likes, shares, and fleeting reactions rather than meaningful understanding or obligation and responsibility.

This insight changed how I thought about even basic features on my site.I began to question whether this type of engagement truly invited users to reflect or care about the content. True responsibility, as Silverstone emphasized, It’s not just about give-and-take or numbers. True responsibility means caring no matter what, even if nothing comes back in return.

Another important idea from Silverstone’s work is the need for hospitality. Online, this means making room for people who may not reply, agree with us, or fit in with the mainstream. An ethical internet isn’t based on the idea of “I’ll listen to you because you listened to me.” Instead, it’s about choosing to recognize and include others no matter how they respond. This is especially important when we design for accessibility and inclusion. We need to make sure people who are often left out because of disability, language, internet access, or social barriers can not only use digital spaces but also feel truly welcomed in them.

So what does an ethical internet look like to me today? It is a space that is inclusive by design, where difference is not only tolerated but welcomed. It is an environment where users are encouraged to slow down, reflect, and engage thoughtfully, rather than being swept up in automated feeds or manipulative design patterns. It is built on transparency, where both users and platform owners are accountable for the structures they create and sustain. Most importantly, it is a space where care is built into the very architecture where hospitality, not just connection, is the guiding principle.

References:

Orgad, S. (2007) ‘The internet as a moral space: the legacy of Roger Silverstone’, New Media & Society, 9(1), pp. 33–41. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1177/1461444807075202.
