Jumping into the new year

As the first week of the new school year comes to a close, I find myself feeling both anxious and excited. The past week’s lectures have sparked engaging discussions, particularly about the evolution of the internet—how it continues to change, sometimes for the better and sometimes for the worse. One recurring topic was the impact of generative AI on our industry. There was a strong debate about whether AI could replace human skill sets, potentially reducing the need for workers in the coming years. However, it was also noted that AI lacks the ability to critically think and create art that resonates emotionally with people. This disparity presents an opportunity for us, the new generation, to bridge the gap by combining technical expertise with human creativity. Reflecting on what I hope to gain from this course, I realize that my engineering background has shaped the way I think—requiring precision, directness, and a structured approach to presenting information. However, this course challenges me to take a more reflective and expressive approach, one that aligns more with a writer’s perspective. My goal is to develop a well-balanced set of critical thinking skills that merge the logical mindset of an engineer with the creativity of an artist. With that being said, much of the work in this course also delves into the technical aspects of website creation. This includes an in-depth exploration of both front-end and back-end development, allowing us to refine our coding skills and take them to the next level. Additionally, this presents an opportunity to build a digital portfolio—something that could serve as a valuable reference for future job opportunities. Which serves my main purpose for this year as a whole. Which would be, being ready for the workspace after my degree.

Reading

"As we may think"at 65 "As We May Think at 65" is an extension of Vannevar Bush’s visionary 1945 paper, As We May Think. It reflects on Bush’s legacy, emphasizing how his groundbreaking innovations and revolutionary ideals continue to resonate in the modern era. From my understanding of both papers, Harper provides a perspective that praises Bush for developing analog-based concepts that laid the foundation for today’s web. Harper specifically highlights Bush’s emphasis on "association"—the idea that information should be organized through interconnected links rather than rigid hierarchical structures like alphabetical order or digital algorithms. Bush’s vision was not necessarily about creating a digital system but rather an analog construct where information could be dynamically linked, allowing knowledge to build upon itself. His goal was to design a system that enhances human cognition and facilitates learning through interconnected knowledge. Harper extends Bush’s vision by advocating for the development of systems that mimic the associative pathways of the human mind. By doing so, he argues, we can better manage vast amounts of information and ultimately foster higher levels of human intelligence and relinquish false information which in turn ultimately fits perfectly with todays society.

[1] Harper, S. (2010) ‘“As We May Think” at 65’, ACM SIGWEB Newsletter, 2010(Spring), pp. 1–3. [2] Bush, V. (1945) ‘As We May Think’, The Atlantic, 1 July.
