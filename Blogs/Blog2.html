Blog 2: Intro to the World Wide Web & Website Development

24 February 2025, 17:00

URL Scheme and Folder structure

This week, we began a simple introduction in the HTML language and website development. We touched base on "What is a website" and "What are the components that create what we know as a website". A consistent word was repeatedly brought up this week that properly dissects the answers to these queries. The word was "Wireframe" which was explained as the outline and layout of the "Website" in which the difference between a website and web page was illustrated. In this, the purpose of HTML was brought to light as it was explained as the creation of structure of the website and not the styling. It was referred to as the bones (Human body) of the website. This alludes to how the use of HTML creates the main foundation of your website and how it initializes the various information from the folders created. This led to conversations about folders and how in industry projects are preferred to be laid out in a neat and orderly fashion. This was to be implemented in our own project folders, in which different designated information should be placed in their own folders underneath the project folder. In this, my folder structure was laid out in this specific format.

  • Blogs – Weekly blog posts, stored as index HTML files with names relative to the blog post
  • Images – Where PNG files of our images will be saved
  • Scripts – JavaScript code files
  • Styling – CSS files for the aesthetic of the website
  • index.html – Our home page with an introduction about who I am

In terms of the URL scheme, I didn't do too much because lucky for us, GitHub handles the URL scheme and provides the necessary license we require. If we didn't have it, I would need a deeper dive in URL scheme as a concept to initialize the website as its own identity.

'You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media (1991)' - Moulthrop, S.

The essay created by Moulthrop entails the creation of the Xanadu by Ted Nelson as a linkage and read-only system. He frames it as a digital equivalent of a library but on a global and interlinked stage. He continues to reference Bush and his ideals and vision of memex to further elaborate the creation of the web and traces the very steps that led to its invention. He does this to elaborate the historical information of hypertext and growth of interactivity in general. He further extends on the impact of hypertext in context of "Laws of Media" by McLuhan, which questions:

  • Its ability to enhance
  • What does it render obsolete
  • What does it revive/retrieve that was previously obsolete
  • What happens when it is taken to its limits

With context to these questions, Moulthrop's essay elaborates that hypertext creates and alludes to an interconnected network similar to fast food chains in which retrieving and linking information occurs in an improved way. A way in which readers may connect different ideas to different texts and information and overall be on a larger platform for interactivity. He also mentions how the effect of hypertext may create a downfall for print culture which may be referred to as "the death of the book". In this way, this alludes to a change in the traditional way of interacting with media, creating a new one with passive media mixed with active engagement. He then further extends his points by mentioning that hypertext brings back older means of information engagement. He emphasizes that the knowledge may be passed on the premise of linked and associative text and commentary provided by user inputs, hence connectivity through ideas evolves. He also further extends on the consequences and the need for control in this future venture. Although Moulthrop wrote this during the early development of the web, his concerns are still highly relevant. His warning that hypertext might be taken over by corporations has become a reality, as seen in the dominance of tech giants like Google and Facebook. However, the growth of open-access platforms shows the democratic element he imagined, showing that hypertext can still serve for broader access to information.
